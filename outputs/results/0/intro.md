# Related Works

This section reviews prior work across three domains: agent-based economic modeling, agent-based tax policy evaluation, and LLM-driven simulation.

## Agent-Based Economic Modeling

Agent-based modeling (ABM) has become a cornerstone of computational economics, enabling the simulation of complex macroeconomic phenomena from micro-level interactions. The ABIDES framework provides a foundational agent-based simulator for economic systems, supporting high-fidelity market modeling with heterogeneous agents [1]. Building on this, ABIDES-Economist extends the framework with learning agents capable of adaptive behavior [2]. Reinforcement learning techniques have further enhanced agent-based market simulations, enabling agents to exhibit more realistic stylized behaviors [3]. These advances establish ABM as a rigorous methodology for macroeconomic policy evaluation.

## Agent-Based Tax Policy Evaluation

A separate line of research applies agent-based methods to tax compliance and policy design. Early work modeled income tax evasion as an emergent phenomenon arising from agent interactions [4]. Subsequent research introduced reinforcement learning behavioral models into policy-focused ABM, enabling agents to learn optimal responses to tax policies [5]. Tax compliance has also been studied through an econophysics lens, revealing how public goods provision interacts with taxpayer behavior [6]. Recent work has examined how tax credits impact household economic decisions within simulated learning-agent economies [7], while other studies have modeled taxpayer deduction behaviors and the endogenous probability of tax penalties [8]. These studies collectively demonstrate the value of agent-based approaches for understanding heterogeneous taxpayer responses.

## Economic Inequality and Fiscal Policy

Agent-based models have also been employed to study economic inequality and fiscal policy interventions. An agent-based model for poverty and discrimination policy-making evaluates the mechanisms through which inequality persists [9]. Research on inequality and financial accumulation provides computational analysis of how wealth distribution evolves [10]. More recently, network-based models have examined homophilic effects on economic inequality, showing how social network homophily can amplify income disparities [11]. While these studies offer valuable insights, they have generally not leveraged LLMs for policy optimization.

## LLM-Driven Agents in Economic Simulation

An emerging area explores the use of large language models as agents within economic simulations. The Focus Agent framework demonstrates how LLM-powered agents can simulate realistic group decision-making processes [12], suggesting the potential for LLMs to serve as behavioral models in macroeconomic simulations. While prior work has applied LLMs to individual economic decisions and policy reasoning, the integration of LLM-driven government agents with heterogeneous household agents for iterative tax policy optimization remains underexplored.
