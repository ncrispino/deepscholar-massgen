# Related Works

The intersection of taxation, economic inequality, and computational modeling has received substantial attention in recent decades. This section reviews relevant literature across optimal taxation theory, agent-based economic simulations, and the emerging application of large language models in economic modeling.

## Optimal Taxation and Redistribution Models

Classical optimal taxation theory seeks to design tax schedules that balance efficiency with equity under informational constraints [1][2]. Research on wealth-based versus exchange-based tax systems found that the choice of tax base significantly impacts distributional outcomes [3]. Studies on growth-optimal tax rates have shown that the trade-off between inequality reduction and economic growth varies with specific tax instrument designs [4]. Recent work on wealth inequality and utility has examined how different redistribution approaches affect consumption patterns and welfare [5]. An agent-based approach to wealth inequality that includes fiscal evasion dynamics has provided insights into how complex behavioral factors interact with tax policy [6].

## Agent-Based Economic Modeling

Agent-based modeling (ABM) offers a flexible framework for simulating complex economic systems with heterogeneous agents. The ABIDES-Economist framework introduced agent-based simulation of economic systems with learning agents, demonstrating how individual decision-making behaviors aggregate to produce macroeconomic phenomena [7]. An agent-based model for poverty and discrimination policy-making has been developed to evaluate interventions at the systemic level [8]. Research on wealth inequality using artificial chemistry models has shown how tax policies influence the distribution of wealth through exchange dynamics [9].

Studies on taxpayer behavior have highlighted the importance of modeling heterogeneous responses to taxation. Research on taxpayer deductions and the endogenous probability of tax penalization demonstrated that individuals exhibit varying compliance behaviors that significantly affect revenue outcomes [10]. A policy-focused agent-based modeling approach using reinforcement learning behavioral models has been proposed to evaluate fiscal policy interventions [11]. A multi-level multi-agent simulation methodology has been developed to engineer and validate dynamic economic models [12]. The heterogeneity in taxpayer responses represents a key limitation of traditional optimal taxation models, which often assume homogeneous behavioral responses.

## Large Language Models in Economic Simulation

The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has opened new possibilities for economic simulation. Recent work has explored using LLMs as agents within social simulations, demonstrating their potential to capture nuanced decision-making processes [13]. The concept of exploiting language models as a source of knowledge for cognitive agents has been proposed, suggesting that LLMs can provide rich semantic understanding of economic contexts [14]. Role-playing simulations using ChatGPT have shown promise in modeling complex social interactions, pointing toward the potential for LLM-based agents to simulate taxpayer and government behaviors [15].

## Integrating LLMs with Agent-Based Modeling for Policy Design

Despite advances in both optimal taxation theory and agent-based modeling, significant gaps remain. Traditional optimal taxation models often fail to capture the heterogeneity and bounded rationality of real-world taxpayers. Existing agent-based models provide rich micro-foundations but typically rely on simplified behavioral rules. The integration of LLMs with agent-based modeling represents a promising frontier that could address these limitations by enabling more sophisticated, context-aware behavioral modeling within established simulation frameworks.
